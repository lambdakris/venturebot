# The Trip Planning Process: Complete Workflow

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** 2026-01-05
**Based On:** Mount Yale winter climb walkthrough and user expertise
**Purpose:** Document the actual process of planning mountaineering trips for agent development

---

## Overview: Two-Phase Planning

Trip planning naturally divides into two distinct phases:

1. **META PLANNING** - Creating reusable "objective blueprints" for peak/route/season combinations
2. **EVENT PLANNING** - Planning specific attempts with temporal details

Each phase has different information sources, decision points, and timelines.

---

## Phase 0: Discovery & Selection (Pre-Meta Planning)

**When:** When you don't yet have a specific objective in mind

**Purpose:** Find and select an objective that matches your criteria

### Process

**Step 1: Define Criteria**
- Skill level (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
- Season (winter, spring, summer, fall)
- Location (Colorado, PNW, etc.)
- Hazard preferences (avoid avalanche terrain, avoid exposure, etc.)
- Other constraints (distance from home, accessibility, etc.)

**Step 2: Survey Available Objectives**
- **Primary resources:**
  - 14ers.com (for Colorado)
  - SummitPost (broader coverage)
  - Guidebooks (Roach's Colorado 14ers, etc.)
  - Web searches for regional resources

- **Search methods:**
  - Keyword searches ("winter 14ers avoid avalanche terrain")
  - Browse by region/difficulty
  - Articles and curated lists (e.g., "winter 14ers for beginners")

**Step 3: Compare Options**
- Difficulty (class rating, technical requirements)
- Accessibility (trailhead access, permits, closures)
- Safety (avalanche terrain, exposure, objective hazards)
- Logistics (distance, typical duration)

**Step 4: Select Objective**
- Choose one that best matches criteria
- Document rationale for selection

### Example: Mount Yale Selection

**Criteria:** "14ers in Colorado, winter routes, avoid avalanche terrain, entry-level"

**Survey:** Web search ‚Üí 14ers.com winter beginners article

**Options Found:** Yale, Quandary, others

**Selection:** Mount Yale East Ridge - Winter
- **Rationale:**
  - Entry-level winter 14er
  - East Ridge avoids avalanche terrain
  - Trailhead typically accessible in winter
  - Documented route with good information

## Phase 1: Meta Planning - Creating the Objective Blueprint

**When:** After selecting an objective, before planning a specific attempt

**Purpose:** Gather stable, reusable information about the objective

**Output:** Meta plan document (e.g., "Mount Yale East Ridge - Winter.md")

### Step 1: Initial Research - Route & Access

**Information to gather:**
- Route description (turn-by-turn)
- Distance and elevation gain
- Class/difficulty rating
- Trailhead location and access
- Season-specific considerations
- Typical timing estimates

**Information sources (by priority):**

1. **Established route databases:**
   - 14ers.com route pages (general route info)
   - 14ers.com trailhead pages (access, winter accessibility)
   - SummitPost (route descriptions)
   - Mountain Project (technical routes)

2. **Season-specific articles:**
   - 14ers.com winter articles (limited coverage)
   - Blog posts and articles (broader coverage)
   - Guidebook winter sections

3. **Trip reports (for META patterns):**
   - Look for consistent patterns across multiple reports
   - Typical conditions, typical gear, typical timing
   - Known hazards and challenges

**Key insight:** Information is often fragmented across multiple pages/sources. You must:
- Know to look for trailhead pages separately from route pages
- Search for season-specific articles beyond main route page
- Synthesize information from disparate sources

**For Mount Yale East Ridge - Winter:**
- Route page: https://www.14ers.com/route.php?route=yale2 (general route)
- Trailhead page: https://www.14ers.com/php14ers/trailheadsview.php?thparm=sw23 (winter access!)

### Step 2: Hazard Assessment

**Identify typical hazards for this objective/season:**

**General mountaineering hazards:**
- Exposure (fall potential)
- Rockfall
- Route-finding difficulty
- Weather exposure
- Commitment level
- Altitude/elevation

**Season-specific hazards:**
- Winter: Cold, wind, avalanche, shorter days, snow obscuring route
- Spring: Wet avalanches, postholing, stream crossings
- Summer: Lightning, afternoon storms, heat, crowding
- Fall: Early winter storms, unpredictable conditions

**Route-specific hazards:**
- Technical sections (rappels, exposed scrambling)
- Loose rock
- Water crossings
- Glacier hazards (if applicable)

**Avalanche terrain assessment (critical for winter/spring):**
- Does route cross avalanche terrain?
- Can avalanche terrain be avoided?
- What aspects are exposed to avalanche hazard?
- Are there safe alternatives?

**Classify the objective:**
- Class rating (YDS: Class 1-5)

- Fitness requirements (distance, elevation gain)
- Experience requirements (navigation, winter skills, etc.)

**Document turn-by-turn route:**
- Starting point
- Key waypoints
- Navigation challenges
- Where to go, where NOT to go
- Landmarks and decision points

**Season-specific route variations:**
- Winter: Stay on ridge vs summer trail variation
- Spring: Avoid snow-loaded slopes
- Summer: Standard trail vs scrambling variations

**Bailout options:**
- Where can you safely retreat?
- Alternative descent routes
- Decision points for bailing

**Estimate typical times:**
- Segment-by-segment timing
- Total duration range
- Factors that affect timing (snow depth, fitness, conditions)

**Identify decision points:**
- Where should you assess conditions?
- Where are turnaround points?
- What's a reasonable turnaround time?

## Phase 2: Event Planning - Planning the Specific Attempt

**When:** After meta plan exists, when planning a specific attempt

**Purpose:** Apply temporal information to plan a safe, realistic attempt

**Output:** Event plan document (e.g., "Mount Yale East Ridge - Winter - 2026-01-17.md")

### Step 1: Set Basic Parameters

**Define the attempt:**
- Specific date
- Participants (names, skills, emergency contacts)
- Logistics (carpool time/location, trailhead time)

**For Mount Yale - Jan 17, 2026:**
- Date: January 17, 2026
- Participants: TBD
- Carpool: 4:30 AM at TBD location
- Trailhead: 7:00 AM at Avalanche TH

### Step 2: Assess Team Capability

**Match team to objective:**
- Do participants have required skills?
- Fitness level appropriate?
- Experience with this type of objective?
- Comfort with hazards (exposure, avalanche terrain, etc.)?

**This is EVENT because it's specific to WHO is going:**

**Decision gate:** If team doesn't match objective, either:
- Choose different objective (back to Discovery)
- Add training/preparation
- Recruit different/additional team members

### Step 3: Conditions Monitoring & Assessment

**This is the most complex and sophisticated part of event planning.**

#### Timeline-Based Monitoring Strategy

**~11+ days out (when objective is selected):**
- Note upcoming storms/weather systems
- Begin casual monitoring
- Not yet actionable for planning

**~10 days out (when forecasts become available):**
- Start checking weather forecasts
- Begin monitoring avalanche forecast trends
- Assess early feasibility
- Consider developing Plan B objectives

**~7-5 days out:**
- More serious monitoring
- Refine understanding of weather pattern
- Check for trip reports post-storm
- Make preliminary Plan B decision if needed

**~3-2 days out:**
- Deep dive on conditions
- Final weather forecast
- Final avalanche forecast
- Search broadly for recent trip reports
- Check quantitative data (SNOTEL, etc.)
- Make final go/no-go decision

**Night before:**
- Final weather check
- Final avalanche forecast check
- Any last-minute trip reports
- Confirm or abort

**Morning of:**
- Final weather check
- Final avalanche forecast check
- Commit or bail

#### Monitoring Intensity by Objective Type

**HIGH Avalanche Concern Objectives** (couloirs, backcountry skiing, steep snow):
- üìä **Intensive monitoring required**
- Track expected AND actual precipitation (snowfall amounts)
- Track wind patterns (speed, direction, duration)
- Model where/how snow was deposited (wind loading on specific aspects)
- Track temperature patterns (freeze-thaw cycles, consolidation potential)
- Validate predictions against CAIC forecast evolution
- Build area-specific mental model of snowpack
- Look for signs of instability (whumpfing, cracking, recent avalanches)
- **Required conditions:** Typically need LOW avalanche danger
- **Abort triggers:** Moderate or higher danger, recent loading, poor consolidation

**LOW Avalanche Concern Objectives** (ridge routes avoiding avy terrain):
- üìä **Moderate monitoring required**
- Watch for HIGH or EXTREME avalanche danger (still avoid even on low-concern routes)
- Focus on weather for exposure management (cold, wind, precipitation)
- Track storm systems for trail-breaking concerns (massive new snow = exhausting trail breaking)
- Less intensive than high-concern objectives
- **Required conditions:** Not HIGH/EXTREME danger; manageable weather
- **Abort triggers:** HIGH+ avalanche danger, extreme weather (wind chills, whiteout)

#### Information Sources by Category

**Weather Forecasts:**
- NOAA Point Forecast (7-10 day, detailed)
- Mountain Forecast (summit-specific conditions)
- OpenSnow (snowfall forecasts, easy-to-read)
- Weather Underground (hourly details)
- Windy.com (wind patterns visualization)

**Avalanche Forecasts:**
- CAIC (Colorado Avalanche Information Center) - daily updates by 4:30 PM
  - Overall danger rating (below treeline, near treeline, above treeline)
  - Avalanche problems (wind slab, persistent slab, wet loose, etc.)
  - Travel advice
  - Snowpack summary
- Regional forecasts updated daily
- Check for your specific zone (Sawatch, Front Range, etc.)

**Quantitative Snow Data:**
- SNOTEL stations (snow depth, SWE, temperature)
- Extrapolate from nearby stations at similar elevation/aspect
- Track trends over time

**Recent Conditions (Trip Reports):**
- **14ers.com** (primary for Colorado)
  - Trip reports section
  - Peak conditions updates
  - Forum discussions
- **Reddit** (r/Mountaineering, r/14ers, regional subs)
- **Facebook groups** (14ers.com FP group, regional climbing groups)
- **SummitPost** (trip reports)
- **AllTrails** (reviews/conditions)
- **Personal blogs** (thevirtualsherpa.com, many others)
- **Instagram/YouTube** (search hashtags, recent videos)

**Road/Access Conditions:**
- CoTrip.org / CO511 (Colorado road conditions)

#### Handling Incomplete Information

**Reality:** You often won't have perfect information. Options:

**1. Weather-Based Modeling:**
- Study weather history (past week's temps, precip, wind)
- Build mental model of likely conditions
- Forecast forward based on upcoming weather

**2. Historical Comparison:**
- Find trip reports from similar timeframes (same peak, same month, similar snow year)
- Take with grain of salt (this year is not last year)
- Balance with current weather modeling

**3. Quantitative Extrapolation:**
- Use SNOTEL data from nearby stations
- Extrapolate based on elevation/aspect differences
- Understand typical snowpack for the season

**4. Multi-Source Search:**
- Don't limit yourself to one website
- Cast a wide net (14ers.com, Reddit, Facebook, SummitPost, AllTrails, blogs, etc.)
- Recent reports (within 1-2 weeks) are most valuable

**5. Informed Assumptions:**
- Plan for typical winter conditions
- Bring just-in-case items (snowshoes, ice axe, crampons)
- Go with "check and see" mindset
- Be willing to turn back if conditions don't match expectations

#### Understanding Snow Consolidation (Gross Simplification)

**Factors AGAINST consolidation (unstable snowpack):**
- Continued snowfall (loading)
- High winds (wind loading, slab formation)
- Cold temperatures (snow doesn't bond)
- Rapid temperature changes
- No time for settling

**Factors FOR consolidation (more stable snowpack):**
- Time without new loading (several days)
- Freeze-thaw cycles (snow bonds and strengthens)
- Warm days + cold nights
- Gentle warming trend
- Sunshine on slopes

**Critical caveat:** Snow science is HIGHLY complex. These are broad strokes only. Always:
- Contrast your model with CAIC forecast evolution
- Look for expert assessment in avalanche forecasts
- When in doubt, avoid avalanche terrain or abort
- This is NOT a substitute for avalanche education

### Step 4: Detailed Itinerary & Timing

**Create specific timeline:**
- Incorporate actual sunrise/sunset times for the date
- Calculate drive time to trailhead
- Estimate segment times based on:
  - META baseline timing
  - Current conditions (snow depth, trail breaking, etc.)
  - Team fitness
  - Season (winter is slower)
- Set specific turnaround time
  - Based on daylight hours
  - Based on weather window
  - Based on typical timing for route
- Decision points with specific times

**For Mount Yale - Jan 17, 2026:**
- Sunrise: ~7:15 AM
- Sunset: ~5:00 PM
- Carpool: 4:30 AM (allows buffer for delays)
- Trailhead: 7:00 AM (at sunrise)
- Turnaround time: TBD based on conditions, but likely 11 AM - 12 PM
- Return to cars: Target 4:00 PM (before dark)

### Step 5: Gear Finalization

**Start with META baseline gear, adjust for EVENT conditions:**

**Adjustments based on:**
- Specific weather forecast (colder = more layers, wind = goggles/balaclava)
- Snow conditions (deep snow = definitely bring snowshoes, icy = crampons)
- Recent trip reports (what did others need?)
- Team composition (do we have all group gear covered?)

**Assign group gear:**
- Who's bringing rope? (if needed)
- Who's bringing first aid kit?
- Who's bringing InReach/PLB?
- Ensure redundancy for critical items (navigation, first aid, shelter)

**For Mount Yale - Jan 17, 2026:**
- Check forecast for specific temps/wind
- Adjust layers accordingly
- Decide on snowshoes (check recent reports, SNOTEL depth)
- Ice axe + crampons (bringing for practice even if not strictly required)
- Assign group gear once team is finalized

### Step 6: Logistics Coordination

**Confirm specific details:**
- Exact carpool location and time
- Who's driving?
- Trailhead arrival time
- Participant contact info
- Emergency contact info for each participant
- Communication plan (InReach check-ins, etc.)

**Verify access:**
- Is trailhead road actually passable? (check recent reports, road conditions)
- Parking availability?
- Any permits needed? (usually not for day trips, but check)

### Step 7: Final Go/No-Go Decision

**Decision gates:**

**3-2 days before:**
- Weather forecast acceptable?
- Avalanche forecast acceptable?
- Conditions reports acceptable?
- Team still committed and healthy?
- **Activate Plan B if needed**

**Night before:**
- Final conditions check
- Any last-minute changes?
- Confirm or abort

**Morning of:**
- Final weather/avalanche check
- How does everyone feel?
- Commit or bail
- **No shame in bailing - mountains will be there**

**Criteria for GO:**
- Weather forecast manageable (no extreme cold, extreme wind, whiteout)
- Avalanche forecast not HIGH/EXTREME (even for low-concern route)
- Team capable and healthy
- Logistics sorted
- Gut feeling is good

**Criteria for NO-GO:**
- Harsh weather forecast (extreme conditions)
- HIGH or EXTREME avalanche danger
- Team concerns (illness, injury, lack of preparedness)
- Access issues (road closed, parking unavailable)
- Gut feeling says no (trust your instincts)

## Key Decision Points & Iteration

### When Planning Loops Back

**Discovery ‚Üí Selection:**
- If no objectives match criteria, refine criteria or expand search

**Meta Planning ‚Üí Discovery:**
- If objective is too hard/dangerous, go back and select different objective

**Event Planning ‚Üí Meta Planning:**
- If conditions never align for this objective, maybe wrong season/route

**Event Planning ‚Üí Discovery:**
- Most common: conditions poor for this objective, user starts over with different objective using the agent

**Event Planning ‚Üí Discovery:**
- If entire trip window has poor conditions, might need to wait for better season

### Critical Decision Gates

**Gate 1: Objective Selection**
- Does this match my criteria?
- Is it appropriate for my skill level?
- **Abort:** If no, return to discovery

**Gate 2: Team Capability**
- Does the team have required skills?
- **Abort:** If no, change objective or team

**Gate 3: Conditions Assessment (~1 week out)**
- Are conditions trending favorable?
- **Abort:** If no, user can start over with different objective

**Gate 4: Final Go/No-Go (night before / morning of)**
- Weather acceptable?
- Avalanche acceptable?
- Team ready?
- Gut check?
- **Abort:** If any no, postpone or choose different objective

**Gate 5: In-Field Decisions (during the trip)**
- Conditions as expected?
- Team performing well?
- On schedule?
- **Abort:** If no, turn around (this is EVENT-day decision making, not planning)

## Special Considerations

### Winter-Specific Planning

**Additional hazards:**
- Avalanche (primary concern)
- Cold exposure / frostbite
- Shorter daylight hours
- Snow obscuring route
- Trail breaking (exhausting)

**Additional gear:**
- Microspikes, crampons, ice axe
- Snowshoes
- Extra insulation
- Goggles, balaclava
- Insulated water bottles

**Different mindset:**
- More conservative turnaround times
- Higher abort threshold
- Avalanche awareness paramount
- Check and see approach

### High Avalanche Terrain

**If objective crosses avalanche terrain:**
- Formal avalanche education REQUIRED
- Intensive conditions monitoring
- Build mental model of snowpack
- Typically need LOW danger
- Field observations and decisions critical
- Rescue gear required (beacon, probe, shovel)

### Technical Routes

**If objective requires technical skills:**
- Rope systems
- Anchors and protection
- Rappel procedures
- Partner skills matched
- Group gear coordinated
- Rescue plan established

## Agent Implications

**The agent needs to:**

1. **Understand the two-phase structure** (meta vs event)
2. **Guide discovery** (help find objectives matching criteria)
3. **Synthesize fragmented information** (route pages + trailhead pages + articles)
4. **Model conditions** (not just look up, but reason about weather/snow)
5. **Search broadly** (many sources for trip reports and beta)
6. **Differentiate monitoring strategy** (high vs low avalanche concern)
7. **Build timelines** (what to check when)
8. **Apply judgment** (when information is incomplete)
9. **Provide nuanced guidance** (consolidation dynamics, abort criteria)
10. **Embrace uncertainty** (check and see mindset, be willing to turn back)
11. **Track over time** (monitor forecast evolution, not single snapshot)

**Note:** Plan B/C/D development is **user responsibility**. If conditions don't work for an objective, the user simply uses the agent again to plan a different objective. The agent focuses on planning ONE objective well.

## Conclusion

Trip planning is **sophisticated, iterative, and requires judgment**. It's not a simple lookup task.

The agent must:
- Understand mountaineering principles
- Synthesize multi-source information
- Model and reason about conditions
- Provide context-aware guidance
- Adapt to objective type (avy concern, difficulty, season)
- Support decision-making over time

This process document captures the real workflow of experienced mountaineers and provides the foundation for building an agent that truly understands trip planning.
